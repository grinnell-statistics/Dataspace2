 Are you Pychic?

Understanding p-values in research studies

Contributors: Shonda Kuiper , DASIL

Part 3 Introduction

Imagine playing a card game with a friend. In the game there are 5 cards in front of you and your friend in his mind chooses one of them. Your task is to use your psychic abilities to guess which one it is, and then select it. If you get it correctly, then you get a point. The game goes for 25 rounds and in the end your score is calculated. You don’t believe in psychic abilities, but your friend just got 15 out of 25 correctly, compared to you with only 5 guessed. You may say it is luck, someone else may say that the friend is actually psychic, and you are too grumpy to admit that. To see what is the truth you may want to calculate the p-value that will determine what is the probability of the friend getting this high score if he is a regular, non-psychic player. However, before getting more into the topic of p-value, let’s spend a minute discussing hypothesis testing, which always goes together with our p-value.


When reading about p-values you will encounter the name of “hypothesis testing”, that is a method used to decide if the data we work with supports an event we are interested in. In hypothesis testing there are two of these events called “null hypothesis” and “alternative hypothesis”, that contradict themselves, which is why only one can be true. The null hypothesis, as the name suggests, is a status quo, and hypothesizes that there is no difference. On the other hand, the alternative hypothesis proposes a change, a difference.


For example, if discussing the harmfulness of smoking, the null hypothesis would state that it does not lower life expectancy, while the alternative hypothesis would say the contrary – that it indeed does lower it. Or for the case of psychic game:

  • Null Hypothesis (H₀): The friend that got a high score in the game was just lucky
  • Alternative Hypothesis (H₁): The friend that got a high score in the game is actually psychic

    • In order to evaluate which one is true we need to calculate the p-value, that is a number between 0 and 1. The higher the value the higher the probability of a certain result if the null hypothesis is true. In our example, the p-value is the probability of the friend who is a normal player (just lucky) to get a score that is as high as that of someone who is actually psychic. You can probably see that in order to determine that the friend is psychic, we would like this probability to be as low as possible. The lower it is, the lower the probability that he is just an average player who got lucky. The standard used in the scientific world as a cut-off p-value is 0.05. That means that we consider p-value of 0.05 to be low enough to “reject the null hypothesis” and “support alternative hypothesis” i.e. have evidence that the friend is psychic. However, if the number is higher than 0.05, we “fail to reject the null hypothesis” and “cannot support alternative hypothesis”, as we simply cannot tell if the friend is psychic or just got lucky.

    Before the game begins, you will be asked to select:

  • The number of cards you would like to play with (the cards you will be guessing from)

    1B: Collecting Data

    The Psychic Game is intended to show that event when using the benchmark of p-value of 0.05, we are likely to get false-positive results. Such false-positives ocurr when we think that the result of the study is statistically significant while it actually is just "lucky". While playing the game pay attention to the p-value of the scores you get and notice how it fluctuates across the games you play. How does it compare to the average? How close it is to the games of the highest-scoring students in your group?
