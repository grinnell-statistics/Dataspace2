Exploring Racial Disparities in New York City's Stop-and-Frisk Policies

by Shonda Kuiper and Yusen He

Introduction

Many individual cases have been found to show evidence of discrimination by police officers. However, showing evidence of systematic patterns (e.g. clear evidence of bias across an entire city) is more difficult. Before drawing conclusions with any dataset, it is essential to consider the type of data available, how the data was collected, and how the data was organized.

Every year, the New York City Police Department (NYPD) stops individuals for suspected criminal involvement. From 2002 to the first quarter of 2015, there were more than five million stop-and-frisk interrogations (New York Civil Liberties Union, 2015). This lab will use data from the New York City Police Department to describe how easy it is to draw incorrect conclusions from these types of data.

The emphasis is not on conducting a single statistical test that provides one undisputable answer. Instead, exploratory data analysis, visualization and modeling should be used to make decisions with complex and messy data.

Comparing Counts and Percentages

For many years, the New York City Police Department (NYPD) stopped individuals for suspected criminal involvement. If police thought anyone appeared to be suspicious, they had the right to stop and then frisk them. These stop‐and‐frisk policies were an effort to reduce crime rates, under the philosophy that preventing smaller crimes will reduce the likelihood of more violent crimes (Associated Press, 2009).

In 2009, New York Police Department Commissioner Raymond Kelly said, “This is a proven law enforcement tactic to fight and deter crime, one that is authorized by criminal procedure law (Long 2009).” However, in recent years, the NYPD has been accused of being racially discriminatory in their use of this policy.

The graphs below visualize the same data in two ways. Figure 1A displays the number of arrests based upon the race of the suspect, and Figure 1B displays the percentage of people stopped who were arrested for each race. Figure 1A clearly illustrates that People of Color have many more arrests than Whites. However, when we focus on the percentage of people stopped who were arrested (number of arrests/number of stops for each race), there is no longer a clear racial disparity. By comparing Figure 1A and Figure 1B, we see an example of how the same data can tell different stories depending on the way the data is summarized.

Figure 1A: Bar graph representing the total number of people arrested in New York by race of the suspect from 2006 to 2016. Figure 1B: Bar graph of the (number of people arrested / the number of people stopped) for each race between 2006 and 2016.

KEY IDEA: Whenever we are shown a percentage, we should always ask the question, “Percentage of what?” From Figure 1A, it appears that about 50% of all arrests made in New York involve a Black suspect. This is consistent with previous research stating that, from January 1998 through March 1999, Blacks and Hispanics represented 51% and 33% of the individuals stopped by NYPD, while representing only 26% and 24% of the New York City population (percentages are respective; Gelman, Fagan, & Kiss 2012). Figure 1B answers a different question for each race, "Given that a person has been stopped, what percentage of those stopped will be arrested?" The core issue is that the values chosen can be mathematically accurate, but lead to very different conclusions.
