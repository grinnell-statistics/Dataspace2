The Racer Game Part 3

Introduction

In this lab, your goal is to complete a research study based on the Racer game. Each car you select can be raced multiple times on a track and the time to complete each race will be recorded. After you race the cars, the data will be available for you to analyze.

3A: Designing an Experiment

Many people assume that statistics focuses on calculations. However, most real-world projects spend much more time properly collecting data (or cleaning/organizing data) than they do on the calculations. For example, before collecting any data for an experiment involving the Racer game, the following steps should be addressed:

  1. Develop a hypothesis about the population of all races. Here are a few examples:
    2. • I believe that on average, Bayes car is faster than the Gauss Car
    • I believe that on average, the Gauss car will complete a race faster than the Nightingale car.
    • I believe that at least one of the average Finish Times is different for the Bayes, Gauss, or Nightingale cars.
  2. Determine how you will collect your sample
    3. • What will you consider as an observation? For example, will it be a player, an individual race, a pair of races, a car, or a track?
    • How many samples will you collect?
    • What is the explanatory variable? Is it categorical or quantitative?
    • What is the response variable? Is it categorical or quantitative?
    • How will randomization be incorporated into the data collection?
    • What specific data needs to be recorded with each race?
    • What graphs or statistical methods do you plan to use once you collect the data? In the example below, we discuss using a paired t-test.
  3. Identify potential confounding variables. In order to evaluate whether the car speeds are truly different, we should take into account possible confounding variables (variables that the researcher did not include in the study but that might be connected to both the independent variable and the dependent variable). For example, some racetracks are longer than others. We want to hold the track constant for everyone in your experiment to ensure that the type of track does not bias our results. Are there any other potential confounding variables that might get in the way of determining which car is faster?

3B: Collecting Data

Researchers need clear protocols (instructions) so that the experimental data is collected using the same process every time. Documentation of these protocols also enables other researchers to verify the validity of the data. If collecting data as a class, clearly identify the exact protocols (instructions) and timeframe for collecting data. For example, here are the protocols for data collected in an earlier class:

  • All data was collected during class time on a computer in the statistics lab room.
  • Students logged onto the site and went to the Racer game.
  • Make sure you enter the correct  GroupID and PlayerID.
  • Click the  Paired Test  button on the main menu.
  • Each student in the class will race exactly two times on the Oval Track. Prior to racing the cars, students were randomly assigned which car they would race first.
  • Use the  pull down menu to select which car you will race first.
  • Then select  which car you will race second.
  • Then select the  Track.
  • Then click the  Continue  button.
  • Use the arrow keys to race the car.

    • 3C: Examining the Data

    Before conducting any calculations, it is essential to examine your data. For example, the following questions should be asked for any data that was collected in the Racer game.

    1. Were there any players who did not properly follow instructions? Did they play the game an incorrect number of times? Did they use the wrong cars or tracks? Can their data be included?
    2. Were there any outliers or skewed data? How does this influence our analysis? Should these outliers be removed?
    3. How can we account for player skill level?
    4. How do we account for the influence of order in which cars are raced? For example, would you expect players to perform better after practicing with the first car?
    5. How does the variation in effect size (the difference between means) compare to the random variation in our data?
    6. Are there any other issues that may cause our data to be unreliable or invalid?
    7. What conditions are required to conduct a statistical analysis? How can you evaluate whether these conditions were met. If the sample size is small, it is particularly important to verify that the conditions are satisfied. What should you do if the needed conditions are not met?
